Scoring Methodology

Four layers, in order. Each layer has one job. If you're reading this cold weeks from now, start here.
{# ── At-a-glance ───────────────────────────────────────────── #}

At a glance

  1. 1
    Auto-reject gate
    Sector blocklist + your configured salary floor. Triggers short-circuit the rest to 0.0.
  2. 2
    Match to candidate (−4.0 to +4.0, highest weight)
    One Gemini call grounded in the Accomplishments Inventory. Returns evidence rows, mismatches, resume bullets, and cover-letter hooks.
  3. 3
    LLM qualitative nudge (±1.5)
    Role-shape vibe check: IC vs leadership balance, authority, reporting line, strategy-vs-execution skew.
  4. 4
    Adjustment weights (deterministic, additive)
    Substring signals on the JD: tech stack, greenfield, pricing, sector, remote, churn. Fast and brittle — kept on purpose.
Inbound sources
The Score a Job form — paste a URL or the JD text. Scored end-to-end on creation.
Hunt sources

{# ── Why this exists ───────────────────────────────────────── #}

Why the methodology was rewritten

The previous engine scored how appealing a role looked in the abstract — greenfield language, AI-native sector, modern stack — and barely scored how well Jeff matches it. The LLM layer received a 4-line candidate summary and was allowed to nudge by ±1.0. That is not AI scoring. That is a spreadsheet with a chat window.

The fix: a new Layer 2 — Match to Candidate that compares the JD against Jeff's full corpus (resume, LinkedIn, leadership deck, blog posts, and the Accomplishments Inventory) and produces an evidence-grounded match score worth up to ±4.0. The rest of the engine keeps doing what it did, but now it's anchored to a real candidate-vs-role comparison.

Layers run in this order:

{# ── Architecture overview ─────────────────────────────────── #}

The four layers, in order

Final Score = base (3.5) + Layer 4 + Layer 2 + Layer 3, clamped to [0.0, 10.0] at the end, rounded to one decimal place. The clamp happens once, after every layer has been added — a strong match can rescue a so-so role, and a weak match can sink one that looks flashy. Layer 4's positive contribution is capped at +2.5, so the deterministic ceiling is 6.0: no role clears the top tiers on surface signals alone — it has to earn them through Layer 2 evidence.

Top-band gate: any final score above 7.0 must also pass five must-haves — compensation on target trajectory, a genuine leadership seat (not IC), an acceptable or preferred sector, a real build/strategic mandate, and strong specific Layer 2 evidence. If any fail, the score is clamped to 7.0. An 8+ is rare and earned by design.

L1Auto-Reject
Runs first. Blocked sectors and the salary floor. If any trigger, score = 0.0 and the rest of the pipeline is skipped.
L2Match to Candidate (highest weight, −4.0 to +4.0)
One Gemini call. Compares JD requirements against Jeff's Accomplishments Inventory and differentiators. Returns evidence rows (JD requirement → matched accomplishment), explicit mismatches, tailored resume bullets to swap in, and 1–3 cover-letter hooks. This is the layer that answers "is Jeff actually qualified for this?"
L3LLM Qualitative Adjustment (−1.5 to +1.5)
A second LLM call focused on role shape: IC vs leadership balance, authority, reporting line, strategy-vs-execution skew, and culture/business-model red flags. Answers "is the role actually as good as it looks?"
L4Adjustment Weights (deterministic, additive)
Substring keyword matching on the JD: tech stack, greenfield vs. process-upcycling language, pricing model, sector, work arrangement, plus founder type and churn signals pulled from company research. Fast, transparent, brittle — kept on purpose as a fixed signal layer alongside the LLM judgments.

Layer 1 — Auto-Reject (hard dealbreakers)

Salary Floor

$175,000 base minimum. Extracted via regex from JD text. Below floor → auto-reject. If no salary is detected, the role is NOT rejected — benefit of the doubt is given (many strong roles never list salary).

{# ── Layer 2: Match to Candidate ───────────────────────────── #}

Layer 2 — Match to Candidate (−4.0 to +4.0)

This is the highest-weight layer and the one that makes the engine actually score the candidate against the job. One Gemini call takes three inputs:

  1. Candidate profile — preferences, identity, target titles, comp floor (from candidate_profile.yaml).
  2. Candidate corpus — the parsed data/Accomplishments_Inventory.docx: every metric, achievement, and bullet from resume, LinkedIn, leadership deck, blog posts, and brand-pivot notes, tagged by domain.
  3. The job description — full text (capped at 10k chars).

It returns:

  • match_score — a float in [−4.0, +4.0]. See scale below.
  • match_summary — 2–3 sentences explaining the score in plain language.
  • evidence[] — each entry pairs a specific JD requirement with the corpus bullet that backs it, and rates the strength (Strong / Moderate / Weak).
  • mismatches[] — each entry names a JD requirement and what's missing or thin in the corpus, with severity.
  • differentiator_themes[] — themes in the JD that hit Jeff's moats (finance+ops bridge, AI-native ops, consumption-pricing fluency, systems thinking, GitLab-scale).
  • tailored_bullets[] — 3–5 resume bullets pulled directly from the corpus that should appear on the version of the resume sent to this company.
  • cover_letter_hooks[] — 1–3 lines that could open a cover letter, citing corpus evidence.
Match score scale
+3.0 to +4.0Direct, recent, scaled evidence for the JD's core asks. Multiple Strong matches, no High-severity gaps, a named accomplishment with a metric for each key requirement.
+1.5 to +2.9Solid partial match. Core asks have evidence at smaller scale, in an adjacent domain, or with 1–2 medium gaps.
−1.0 to +1.4Mixed. Some evidence, meaningful gaps on what the JD emphasizes most.
−2.0 to −0.9Mostly mismatch. Core requirements lack corpus support even with a stretch.
−3.0 to −2.1Fundamental mismatch. Role asks for things never done at any scale in any related domain.
−4.0 to −3.1Anti-match. Role directly conflicts with stated preferences or requires skills explicitly excluded from the profile.
If the Inventory docx is missing, Layer 2 contributes 0.0 and surfaces a high-severity mismatch entry pointing at the missing file. The other layers still run. Keep your canonical Inventory wherever you maintain your resume source, and sync it into data/Accomplishments_Inventory.docx on each update.
{# ── Layer 3: LLM Qualitative ──────────────────────────────── #}

Layer 3 — LLM Qualitative Adjustment (−1.5 to +1.5)

Layer 2 asks "does Jeff have what this role needs?" Layer 3 asks "is the role actually shaped the way the title suggests?" One Gemini call returns a ±1.5 nudge plus structured role-shape signals: IC vs leadership balance, expected team size, reporting line, strategy-vs-execution skew, plus the existing pros/cons and tech-stack extraction.

Adjusts UP (+0.5 to +1.5)
  • Real authority — P&L, headcount, strategy ownership
  • High-growth company with modern GTM thinking
  • AI/automation is central to the role, not bolted on
  • Reporting line and team size signal a true leadership seat
Adjusts DOWN (−0.5 to −1.5)
  • Reads as a "firefighter" role — all tactical, no strategy
  • Heavily IC despite a leadership title
  • Company in decline or pivoting away from CS
  • Red flags in culture, leadership, or business model

Layer 3 also extracts pricing model, sector, salary range (if stated), tech-stack guesses (CRM, CS tool, comms, OS, cloud), recommended positioning angle, and whether the role looks like an FDE model — all for display purposes.

{# ── Layer 4: Adjustment Weights ───────────────────────────── #}

Layer 4 — Adjustment Weights (deterministic signals)

Fast substring matching on the JD text plus a few flags from company research. No LLM. Brittle by design — paraphrases will miss, and that's fine because Layer 3's LLM call catches the same ideas semantically. Layer 4 is here for a fixed, auditable signal that doesn't depend on a model.

{# ── Worked example: high match ────────────────────────────── #}

Worked example — high match

VP RevOps role at a Series C AI-native devtools company. Greenfield language, modern stack, Jeff's GitLab CS-Ops-through-IPO arc maps directly to the JD.

Layer 1: pass (no blocked sectors, salary $250k base detected) Layer 4 (Adjustment Weights, deterministic): + 2.0 (greenfield language) + 2.0 (AI-native sector) + 1.0 (Mac/Slack stack) + 1.0 (consumption pricing) + 0.5 (fully remote) = raw +6.5 → capped at +2.5 Layer 2 (Match to Candidate, corpus-grounded): match_score = +3.0 evidence: 7 Strong matches (CS Ops build-out, NRR scaling, exec partnership, forecast accuracy, AI-native workflows, finance+ops bridge, consumption pricing fluency) mismatches: 1 Medium (no explicit "developer-led growth" experience) Layer 3 (LLM Qualitative, role shape): llm_adjustment = +0.6 role_shape: Balanced leadership, reports to CRO, ~12 to lead, Strategy-Heavy Final = 3.5 (base) + 2.5 + 3.0 + 0.6 = 9.6 Top-band gate (>7.0): comp ✓ · leadership seat ✓ · sector ✓ · build mandate ✓ · strong L2 ✓ → passes

Result: 9.6 (Dream Job). The Match Analysis panel on the job detail page exposes the 7 evidence rows, the 1 mismatch, the 3–5 tailored resume bullets to swap in, and a cover-letter hook tying GitLab to this company's stage. Note the Layer 4 cap: surface signals maxed out at +2.5 — the score lives above 7 only because Layer 2 evidence and the top-band gate both cleared.

{# ── Worked example: high look, weak match ─────────────────── #}

Worked example — looks good, doesn't match

A flashy "Founding RevOps Engineer" role at an AI-native devtools shop. Layer 4 lights up like a Christmas tree. But the role is 80% Salesforce admin + Snowflake modeling — work Jeff has directed but not personally executed at depth. The new Layer 2 catches it.

Layer 1: pass Layer 4 (deterministic): raw +6.5 → capped at +2.5 (same signals as above) Layer 2 (match): −2.1 (6 Moderate gaps: SFDC build/admin depth, Snowflake DBT fluency, IC engineering) Layer 3 (LLM): −0.6 (heavily IC despite "founding" framing) Final = 3.5 + 2.5 − 2.1 − 0.6 = 3.3 (Probably Skip)

Result: 3.3 instead of the old engine's 9.5–10.0. The old engine would have wasted a week on this. With the Layer 4 cap and Layer 2 carrying the weight, a role that lights up every surface signal but can't back the actual work drops out of consideration — and the mismatch rows say exactly why.

{# ── Design philosophy ─────────────────────────────────────── #}

Design principles

1. Auto-reject first, match next, vibe last

Layer 1 saves LLM tokens on obvious rejects. Layer 2 (highest weight) is the substantive question: is the candidate qualified? Layer 3 handles role-shape nuance. Layer 4 is the fixed-signal baseline.

2. Evidence over vibes

Layer 2 must cite the corpus literally. "(none found in corpus)" is a legitimate finding. No hallucinated metrics, ever.

3. Clamp once, at the end

The old engine clamped the deterministic layer to [0,10] before adding the LLM, which made any max-deterministic role insensitive to the LLM nudge. New engine adds everything, then clamps.

4. The output is more than a score

Every scored job produces evidence rows, mismatches, tailored resume bullets, and cover-letter hooks. A score tells you whether to apply. The Match Analysis panel tells you how.

5. The Inventory is the heart of Layer 2

If the Accomplishments Inventory drifts from reality, Layer 2 drifts too. Cowork owns updating it whenever a new metric or accomplishment lands. Then sync to data/ and redeploy.
